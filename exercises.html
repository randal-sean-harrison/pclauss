Who are you?
Assigned Aug 21 | Due Aug 23
  • The title says it all: Tell me about you.
  • Please note that while you can certainly mention your family (siblings, parents, etc) if you want to, the vast majority of your focus needs to be on you. Depending on your circumstances, I'd suggest no more than one line or so about them, if you mention them at all.
  • I'd like to learn about you: What are you passionate about? What sorts of things are you involved in now? What did you do in high school? Where are you from? What do you hope to accomplish in your time at ND? etc.
  • Remember, Writing Exercises, unless specified otherwise, need to be the equivalent of at least one full page of single-spaced text with 1" margins--about 540 to 550 words.
Inventing Possibilities
Assigned Aug 28 | Due Aug 30
This exercise can address any or all of the following questions--whatever helps you generate a number of ideas or possibilities for your upcoming essay. (The more you write, the better.)

Also, keep in mind that--perhaps ironically--it's really best if you forget about the upcoming essay while writing this exercise. Just let the ideas flow; don't "self-censor" at this point of the writing process. (A common mistake at this point is to have a good idea, not realize it's a good idea, and not write it down. Trust in the Force.)

  • Some of you are only a few minutes or hours from home; others are much, much farther away from home. As you think about your friends, family, and others you know outside of ND, who comes to mind as memorable or important? What's your best friend like? Who's someone you'd be happy never to see again? Why? If you were to describe that person with just a few adjectives, what adjectives would you use? Why? Can you provide some explanation?
  • If you currently play or have played an organized sport, what were your coaches like? Have you had any especially good or bad ones?
  • What about high school, junior high, or elementary school teachers? Any one stand out as different, quirky, weak, strong . . . ?
  • What kinds of jobs have you had? Was your last job a good place to work? a bad place? Why?
  • What about you? What are some of your best qualities? and/or What are some things about yourself you'd like to change?
  • Think and write for a few moments about your hometown and your favorite or least favorite places: If I were to visit your hometown for a week or so, where would you suggest I go out to eat? What are some of your favorite restaurants?
    • Least favorite? What about parks, places to hang out, etc? Good places? Bad places?
  • What about other topics I'm not thinking of here? Anything else you want to kick around as possibly worth writing about for your essay?
You'll not hand in a paper copy of this exercise but instead will paste (not upload) it into your ePortfolio, in the place for "Writing Exercise #2."

Invent for Upcoming Arguments
Assigned Sept 25 | Due Sept 27

This exercise can address any or all of the following questions--whatever helps you generate a range of possible topics or ideas for the next two essays. (The more you write, the better.)

Also, keep in mind that it's best if you forget about the upcoming essays while writing this exercise. Just let the ideas flow; don't "self-censor" at this point of the writing process.

  • What are some activities, hobbies, jobs, extra-curricular activities, etc. you have experience with?
  • Are you on or have you been on any teams? in any clubs? a service organization? If so, how's that been going lately? Like your involvement? Don't like it? (Or, didn't like it? Did like it?)
  • What kinds of jobs have you had? Do you work anywhere now? If so, what's it like?
  • What do you think about ND so far? When you think back to high school, how does ND compare to what you thought it would be? What are the classes like? What about social, spiritual, or other aspects of ND?
  • When you're sitting in a typical class at ND (e.g., Moreau, Organic Chemistry, etc.), what separates you from your classmates? How are you different? How might your ND experience be different from theirs?
  • What campus issues are you concerned about, involved with, happy about, annoyed by, etc?
  • If you had a magic wand, one you could wave in order to change one thing about Notre Dame, what would you change? Why?
  • Or, related to the above question, if Father Jenkins invited you to his office for one hour, to talk about what is good and bad about Notre Dame, what would you discuss with him?
  • Are there campus issues, events, activities you're excited about, frustrated by, interested in, etc? What are they?
  • What about personal accomplishments, achievements, or other things you're proud of?
Response to "A Class Divided"
Assigned Sept 27 | Due Oct 4

Before completing this exercise, which is worth two Writing Exercises (up to 10 pts), watch the Frontline segments available here: "A Class Divided."

Also read "Racial Bias, Even When We Have Good Intentions."

  • Respond to any or all of the following questions about Jane Elliott's workshop and lessons:
  • What do you think the experiment or process reveals about human nature in general? Is about much more than just eye color or skin color?
    • Does it uncover any deeper issues or truths about people-- issues or truths that cut across all sorts of boundaries (eye color, skin color, gender, etc)?
  • After watching the segments about the children, do you think you'd want your kids to experience this? Why or why not?
  • Anything else you'd like to comment on about the segments? For instance, what issues were discussed in class that you would like to explore more?
  • What did we not discuss in class that is worth considering?

This is the same length as most other Writing Exercises, a minimum of 540-550 words, but it's worth two Writing Exercises since it requires watching all of "A Class Divided."

Aspects of My Local Argument
Assigned Oct 23 | Due Oct 25
First, write your intended situation statement at the top of the entry. Next, use the words in bold below as subheadings in your entry.

  • Audience: What do you know about your audience? What about the audience's values, interests, hobbies, fears, concerns, goals, attitudes toward topic, etc.?

  • Goal-Directed: What do you hope to accomplish, both during your argument and as a result of your argument? Think in terms of frames of mind, not just your ultimate goal. Also, remember the Candy Land analogy, "the stops along the way" to your Ultimate Goal.

  • Supported: What are your experiences with the topic/program/idea/etc? What reasons support your position? What are benefits to your perspective, stance, etc?

  • Multi-Sided: What are drawbacks to your position? What are weaknesses or limits to your argument and/or strengths on the other side? (For instance, why might the audience not want to accept your claim(s) or move toward your goals?
